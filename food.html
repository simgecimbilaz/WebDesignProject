1. Pastizzi

Pastizzi are a beloved traditional Maltese pastry, with a rich history deeply rooted in the culinary heritage of Valletta, Malta's capital. These delectable pastries consist of flaky, buttery layers enveloping a filling of either ricotta cheese or a spiced pea mash.

Renowned for their savory flavors and delightful textures, food are often enjoyed as a snack or light meal, accompanied by a sprinkle of powdered sugar or a drizzle of tomato salsa.

The aroma of freshly baked food wafting through the narrow streets of Valletta is a nostalgic and inviting experience, inviting locals and visitors alike to savor a taste of Maltese tradition and culinary craftsmanship.

2. Ftira

Ftira is a traditional Maltese bread that holds cultural significance in Valletta and throughout Malta. This circular, flatbread is characterized by its soft interior and crispy crust. It serves as a versatile base for various toppings, making it a popular choice for sandwiches and snacks.

Common ftira fillings include tuna, capers, olives, tomatoes, and other Mediterranean ingredients. The preparation of ftira involves a meticulous baking process, resulting in a distinctive texture and flavor.

Often enjoyed as a quick and flavorful meal, ftira represents a cherished element of Maltese culinary heritage, providing locals and visitors with a taste of the island's rich gastronomic tradition.

3. Aljotta

Aljotta refers to a type of fish soup popular in Maltese cuisine. It’s a lemony and garlicky soup that becomes especially popular during Lent when meat is forbidden.This delicious Maltese fish soup has its roots in French bouillabaisse but with the addition of mint, lemon, and rice. It’s made with whole small fish (like rockfish) with the head, tail, and fins intact to achieve as much flavor as possible.

The fish is combined with garlic, fried onions, tomatoes, mint, bay leaves, and rice before being garnished with parsley and a squeeze of lemon juice.When done right, aljotta is one of the most delicious soups in Maltese cuisine.

According to this Times of Malta author, she gauges the quality of new restaurants in Malta by getting a taste of their fish soup.

4. Bigilla

Bigilla is a traditional Maltese dish made with mashed tic beans as its primary ingredient. Known locally as ful ta’ Ġirba, it’s a type of bean that’s very similar to broad beans but smaller, darker, and with a harder skin. Recipes for bigilla vary but it’s typically made with tic beans, olive oil, garlic, herbs, and seasonings.

Depending on the cook, other ingredients like chili, capers, and lemon juice can also be added. The beans are usually soaked in water for at least 24 hours to soften them up before cooking.

5. Imqarrun il-forn

Imqarrun il-forn is a beloved Maltese dish, a baked pasta casserole consisting of penne or rigatoni layered with a flavorful ragù sauce of minced meat, tomatoes, garlic, onions, and herbs.

Topped with a creamy béchamel, it's baked to golden perfection, creating a delicious harmony of hearty and comforting flavors that defines Maltese culinary tradition.

6.Stuffat tal-Fenek (Maltese Rabbit Stew)

Fenkata refers to a traditional Maltese communal meal consisting of rabbit cooked in various ways. Popular Maltese dishes made with rabbit include fenek moqli (fried rabbit with garlic) and spagetti tal-fenek (spaghetti with rabbit sauce), but the most delicious has to be stuffat tal-fenek or rabbit stew. It’s a Maltese national dish made with rabbit, vegetables, and spices simmered in a rich red wine and tomato sauce.

Rabbit has a long history in Malta that goes way back to the Knights of St. John in the mid-16th century. During that time, rabbit was the predominant protein of choice among lower income families because of its price and accessibility.

These days, fenkata isn’t as common as it once was, though it continues to hold a special place in Maltese cuisine and culture. Often prepared at home, rabbit stew and other fenkata dishes are usually reserved for special occasions and family gatherings to celebrate important life events.
